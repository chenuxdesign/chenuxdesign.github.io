In-game help experience design

Role: Lead Interaction Designer | Time Span: 3 Months

Overview

There are different ways for a player to get help about a game, ask friends and family for help, search for help online, post question in a game forum, etc. Wait, how about providing help right in game? It has been an overlooked area for most games because usually during a game development process, providing help content and service is more an afterthought rather an integral part of the process. At EA, I got opportunities to work on a couple of in-game help projects that helped change this pattern.

In the project, I am responsible for interaction and information architecture design.

Battlefield 1 console in-game help design

Design Challenges

It is the first time that standard EA support service is incorporated in a console game. So there wasnâ€™t much existing knowledge and guidelines to build upon and follow. Also the project has a very tight deadline because we want to get it in when the game launches and when millions of players play it. Thus, our goal is to design and ship an easy and smooth in-game support experience based on our experience on help.ea.com and learn from there.

Design Process

Due to the nature of playing games on console (PS4, PS3, XBOX ONE, XBOX 360), clicking and tabbing through from one action to another are the major interactions. On the other hand, typing and interact with browser windows could be pretty unpleasant experience on console devices. So in the design, we kept these in mind and design a flow that is easy to navigate and make selections for console players.

After lo-fi mocks were done, I created interactive prototypes and tested it with serveral internal players. The feedback was overall positive. There were some feedback with the country code dropdown and we adjusted it in the final design.

Final Design


Results and reflection

(1) A few months after launch lots of players have clicked on the Help button and read articles in Battlefield one which is very impressive. The helpfulness score were higher compared to help.ea.com which validate our approach of providing help articles based on context. Many players clicked on Contact Us button but only a very small percentage completed the flow. We believe the major reason is that we didn't provide chat and email functions due to the difficulty of typing with console controler.

(2) The project is overal successful but I still have some questions about how console players perceive in-game help and what they expect it to be. I know we have provided some basic help functions but how we could improve in the future? In the next project, I utilized usertesting.com to interview console players and discovered some interesting patterns.

In-game help study

The goal

The study was to learn about how players perceive and use help in game and what they expect to have for in-game help.

The process

For this study, I used Usertesting.com to collect thoughts from players regarding in-game help. For people who aren't familiar with Usertesting.com, it is an online service that help you get videos of real people speaking their thoughts as they use your websites, mobile apps, or prototypes. The reason for using this tool is mainly because it could reach to a large number of target audience with low cost and very short waiting time. With this tool, I was able to get 50 interview responses within a few hours.

Study results and outcome

I reviewed all the test videos and documented all the results with a spreadsheet. After some deep analysis, I put the study results into an visual format. The results reveals several interesting insights that are different from how we thought about in-game help. It also provides directions that we could work on in future in-game help projects.
